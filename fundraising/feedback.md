# Fundraising Advisor Feedback — Step / NSpace Angel Round

## Website & Product Review

### NSpace (https://nspace.is)
- Landing page is in Vietnamese, targeting Vietnamese learners
- Positioning: "AI-powered vocabulary memorization" with personalized learning paths
- Features listed: smart flashcards, AI-generated vocab games, listening/speaking practice, grammar explanations, chat with translation
- Landing page claims: "10,000+ users", "4.8★ rating", "95% memorization rate"
- Available on both App Store and Google Play
- Company entity: NSPACE TECHNOLOGY PTE. LTD. (Singapore-registered)
- The page is polished but feels like a generic edtech template — nothing visually communicates the "unique deep on-demand vocab drill" technology that's supposedly the core differentiator

**Key concern:** The landing page claims 10,000+ users, but the README states peak was ~3,000 and current active is ~500. This inconsistency is a dealbreaker if an investor checks.

### Step (https://step.is)
- Landing page is in English, clean modern design with a strong tagline: "Learn a language from content you can't stop reading"
- Positioning: Turn entertainment (movies, music, podcasts, stories) into language learning
- Landing page claims: "50,000+ learners", "4.9★ rating"
- The page is minimal — only a hero section with category tags (Movies, Music, Podcasts, Stories) and a CTA
- EN/VI language toggle available

**Key concern:** The README says Step hasn't launched yet and traction is TBD, but the landing page shows "50,000+ learners" and "4.9★ rating." If these are placeholder/aspirational numbers, they must be removed before any investor interaction. If real, they should be front and center in the pitch.

---

## What's Working Well

1. **The pivot narrative is compelling.** You validated core technology with real users on NSpace, identified structural problems (creator-led model = high cost per user, low retention), and built Step around the lesson learned. Angels respect founders who iterate honestly rather than clinging to a failing model.

2. **Strong unit economics story.** NSpace costs money every time a user generates content. Step uses premade content shared across all users — near-zero marginal cost. This is a real structural advantage and easy for investors to understand.

3. **The template leverage model is powerful.** 1 human-designed template curriculum → AI replicates into 1,000s of derivative curriculums. This is a genuine content flywheel: high upfront quality investment, massive downstream output. Easy to pitch, easy to visualize.

4. **Lean use of funds.** $8k/month total burn is extremely capital-efficient. The 24-month runway on $192k shows discipline. Angels appreciate founders who stretch dollars.

5. **Founder background is solid.** MBA (Melbourne Business School) + CS/Econ double major (Gettysburg College) + 4 years as team lead at Epic (a serious enterprise software company). Technical founder who can also speak business — that's a strong profile for a solo founder.

## What Needs Serious Work

### 1. Inconsistent Metrics Across Websites and Pitch Materials
- NSpace landing page: "10,000+ users" → README says peak 3,000, current ~500
- Step landing page: "50,000+ learners, 4.9★" → README says not launched, traction TBD

This is the single fastest way to lose investor trust. An angel will Google your product, see the website, compare it to your deck, and if the numbers don't match, the conversation is over. Fix both sites to reflect reality before any pitch.

### 2. The NSpace Story Is Too Self-Deprecating
Phrases like "retention so low that might as well ignore about this product" and "clearly low product-market fit" are too raw for investor materials. Reframe as:

> "We built NSpace to validate our core AI-driven vocabulary technology. We reached 3,000 users and confirmed strong engagement with our deep vocab drill exercises. However, the creator-led model had structural challenges — high per-user content generation costs and retention issues inherent to the open-ended format. These learnings directly informed Step's design: premade, structured curriculums with near-zero marginal cost."

Same facts, completely different energy.

### 3. Solo Founder Risk Is Unaddressed
You're a single founder asking for $200k. Investors will worry about:
- Bus factor (what happens if you burn out or get sick?)
- Bandwidth (can one person build product, manage content, do marketing, AND fundraise?)
- The key hire is non-technical (Learning Content Manager), so there's no technical co-founder pipeline

Address this head-on in the deck. Options:
- Name advisors or mentors who are involved
- Show that you've shipped everything solo as a strength ("I built and launched two products, both app stores, full-stack, solo")
- Describe your co-founder search or plans for a technical/product co-founder
- Frame the Learning Content Manager hire as your "co-founder equivalent" for the content side

### 4. Valuation Step-Up Needs Justification
$1M cap (pre-seed) → $2M pre-money (angel) is a 2x jump. What justifies it? You need concrete evidence:
- Beta user count and engagement metrics for Step
- Retention data from Step beta (especially since NSpace retention was the problem)
- Qualitative feedback quotes from beta testers
- Waitlist numbers if applicable
- Any revenue or willingness-to-pay signals

"Very good feedback and traction" is not a data point. Quantify everything.

### 5. Target Market Is Too Broad
"People who want to learn a language but lack discipline/time" describes hundreds of millions of people. For an angel round, you need a wedge:
- Who are your first 1,000 paying users, specifically?
- Vietnamese professionals preparing for IELTS?
- Southeast Asian Gen Z who consume English-language entertainment?
- Expats learning local languages?

Pick one beachhead. You can expand later. A narrow, well-defined initial market is more convincing than "everyone."

### 6. No Revenue Model
The README doesn't mention how Step makes money. Angels need to see:
- Pricing model (subscription? freemium? one-time purchase per curriculum?)
- Price point (even a rough one)
- Any early revenue or willingness-to-pay data
- Projected LTV and CAC (even rough estimates)

### 7. No Competitive Positioning
The language learning space is one of the most crowded in consumer apps: Duolingo, Babbel, Busuu, Rosetta Stone, LingQ, Readlang, Lingvist, and dozens more. Your deck needs:
- A clear "why Step is different" that goes beyond "AI-powered" (everyone says that now)
- The entertainment-first angle IS genuinely differentiated — lean into it much harder
- The premade-curriculum-with-AI-replication model is a real structural moat — explain why competitors can't easily copy it

### 8. Learning Content Manager Salary May Be Below Market
$3,000/month gross for someone with IELTS 8.0+, 5+ years teaching experience, user research skills, and ability to work independently is competitive in Vietnam but tight for top talent. Be prepared for investors to ask:
- Can you actually hire this person at this rate?
- What's the ESOP allocation? (Mention a specific percentage range)
- What's the retention plan? (This role is your bottleneck — losing this person sets you back significantly)

### 9. Marketing Budget Lacks Strategy
"$4,000/month — Channels: Digital Ads, Conference/Exhibition Presence" is too vague. Even at angel stage, investors want to see:
- Which platforms? (Meta ads? TikTok? Google?)
- What's your target CAC?
- Why conferences? Which ones? What's the expected ROI?
- Any organic/content marketing strategy? (Language learning is a great space for content marketing)

## Structural Recommendations for the Pitch Deck

1. **Lead with Step, not NSpace.** Step is the product you're raising for. NSpace is backstory — put it in a "our journey" or "what we learned" slide, not the opening.

2. **Show the product.** The Step landing page is clean but generic. Include actual screenshots or a short demo video of the learning experience. Investors want to see what users see.

3. **Build the "content flywheel" slide.** This is your most compelling strategic argument:
   - 1 expert-designed template → AI generates 1,000s of curriculums → covers more niches → better marketing targeting → more users → more learning data → better templates → repeat
   - This loop is your moat. Make it visual and central.

4. **Add a traction slide with real numbers.** Even if small, real numbers beat no numbers:
   - Beta users, daily active users, session length, completion rates, retention curves
   - Qualitative quotes from beta testers
   - App store ratings (if live)

5. **Add a "Why Now" slide.** AI-generated educational content is newly viable. The cost of creating high-quality language learning materials has dropped dramatically. This is a timing argument that resonates with investors in 2026.

6. **Team slide should acknowledge the gap and the plan.** "Solo technical founder who's shipped two products. Raising to hire domain expert #1. Advisory board includes [names]. Co-founder search ongoing for [role]."

---

## Bottom Line

The fundamentals are solid: honest pivot, strong economics, lean operation, capable founder. But the pitch materials need significant tightening around three things: (1) metric consistency and honesty across all touchpoints, (2) specificity on market, revenue, and competition, and (3) addressing the solo founder concern directly. Fix the vanity metrics on both websites immediately — that's the highest-priority item before any investor conversation.
