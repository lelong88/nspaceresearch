# The Little Bookshop by the Sea — Chapters 6–10

## Chapter 6: Quyết Định (The Decision)

### Vocabulary List
`developer`, `offer`, `generous`, `pressure`, `decision`, `hesitate`, `opportunity`, `salary`, `rent`, `profit`, `deadline`, `practical`, `doubt`, `risk`, `future`

---

### Passage 1
**Vocab used: `developer`, `offer`, `generous`**

A week passed. Emma was still in Saltwick. She had not gone back to London yet. She spent her days in the bookshop, reading Gran's notes and organizing the shelves.

Then one morning, she received a phone call. A man named Richard Blake introduced himself. He was a property **developer** from London. He had heard about the bookshop.

"I'd like to make you an **offer**," he said. His voice was smooth and confident. "I want to buy the building and turn it into a holiday rental. Saltwick is becoming popular with tourists."

He named a price. It was very **generous**. More money than Emma had ever had. She could pay off her student loans. She could travel. She could start fresh.

Emma thanked him and said she needed time to think. She put the phone down and stared at the wall. The bookshop suddenly felt very quiet.

---

### Passage 2
**Vocab used: `pressure`, `salary`, `opportunity`**

That afternoon, Emma's boss called from London. His name was David, and he was not happy.

"Emma, you've been gone for two weeks. I need you back. There's **pressure** from the clients. We have deadlines."

Emma closed her eyes. She had almost forgotten about her job. She worked in marketing for a big company. The **salary** was good, but the work was stressful. She sat at a desk for ten hours a day and wrote reports that nobody read.

"This is a real **opportunity** for you," David continued. "We're promoting people this quarter. If you come back now, you could move up."

Emma said she would think about it. After the call, she walked to the harbour and sat on the wall. The sea was grey and calm. She watched the boats move slowly in the water.

Two paths. Two lives. She did not know which one to choose.

---

### Passage 3
**Vocab used: `decision`, `hesitate`, `rent`**

Emma talked to Mrs. Penny about the **decision**. They sat in the tea shop with cups of tea and a plate of scones.

"A holiday rental?" Mrs. Penny said. Her face was serious. "That would change everything. This street doesn't need another rental. It needs the bookshop."

Emma understood. But she also had to be realistic. "I don't know if I can run a bookshop," she said. "I don't know anything about it."

She started to **hesitate**. Every time she thought about staying, she felt excited. But every time she thought about the money, she felt pulled back to London.

"What about the **rent** on my flat in London?" she said. "I'm still paying for it. I can't afford two places."

Mrs. Penny reached across the table and held Emma's hand. "Sometimes the right choice is not the easy one, dear."

---

### Passage 4
**Vocab used: `profit`, `deadline`, `practical`**

Richard Blake called again the next day. He was polite but firm.

"The **profit** you could make from this sale is significant," he said. "Property prices in Cornwall are rising fast. This is the best time to sell."

He gave her a **deadline**. "I need your answer by Friday. I have other properties to look at."

Emma felt the weight of the decision pressing down on her. She tried to be **practical**. She made a list of reasons to sell and reasons to stay. The sell list was longer. It had numbers, facts, logic.

The stay list was shorter. It had only three things: the bookshop, the people, and the feeling she had when she sat in Gran's chair.

She looked at both lists. The numbers made sense. But the feeling was stronger.

Tom came by that evening to check on the window he had fixed. She told him about the offer. He listened quietly.

"What do you want to do?" he asked.

"I don't know," she said.

---

### Passage 5
**Vocab used: `doubt`, `risk`, `future`**

Emma lay awake that night. The bed and breakfast was quiet. She could hear the sea through the open window.

She was full of **doubt**. What if she stayed and the bookshop failed? What if she ran out of money? What if she was making a terrible mistake?

But there was another voice in her head. A quieter one. It said: what if you leave and regret it forever?

Staying was a **risk**. She would give up her job, her flat, her life in London. She would start something completely new in a town where she knew almost nobody.

But when she thought about her **future**, she could not see herself back at that desk in London. She could not see herself writing reports and eating lunch alone. She could see herself here. In the bookshop. With the sea outside and books all around her.

She did not call Richard Blake on Friday. She did not call David either. She was not ready yet. But something inside her had already started to shift.

## Chapter 7: Cơn Bão (The Storm)

### Vocabulary List
`storm`, `damage`, `leak`, `bucket`, `volunteer`, `teamwork`, `rescue`, `soaked`, `candle`, `shelter`, `relief`, `grateful`, `celebrate`, `repair`, `strength`

---

### Passage 1
**Vocab used: `storm`, `damage`, `leak`**

The weather changed on Tuesday. Dark clouds moved in from the sea. The wind grew strong. By evening, rain was falling hard against the windows.

Mrs. Penny came to the bookshop door. "There's a big **storm** coming tonight," she said. "The worst in ten years, they say. Close the windows and stay inside."

Emma locked up the shop and went to the bed and breakfast. She listened to the wind all night. It howled like an animal. The rain hit the roof like stones.

In the morning, she ran to the bookshop. The front looked okay, but inside was a different story. Water was dripping from the ceiling. The **damage** was clear — the old roof had a **leak**. Water was falling onto the books on the top shelves.

"No, no, no," Emma whispered. She grabbed a towel and started pulling books off the wet shelves. But there were too many. She could not do this alone.

---

### Passage 2
**Vocab used: `bucket`, `volunteer`, `teamwork`**

Emma ran next door. Mrs. Penny was already cleaning her own shop. "The bookshop roof is leaking!" Emma said. "The books are getting wet!"

Mrs. Penny put down her mop. "I'll get help."

Within thirty minutes, people started arriving. Tom came first with tools and plastic sheets. Then Jack, moving slowly but determined. Then neighbours Emma had never met — a young couple, an older woman, a teenager with headphones around his neck.

Everyone grabbed a **bucket** or a towel. They formed a line and passed books from the wet shelves to dry tables. Every person was a **volunteer**. Nobody asked for anything. They just came to help.

Emma watched them work together. This was **teamwork** — real teamwork. Not the kind she knew from office meetings. This was people caring about something because it mattered to them.

She picked up a bucket and joined the line.

---

### Passage 3
**Vocab used: `rescue`, `soaked`, `candle`**

The work took hours. They moved hundreds of books. Some were already wet. Tom put plastic sheets over the shelves to stop more water from coming in.

They managed to **rescue** most of the collection. The rare books in the locked room were safe — the ceiling there was solid. But about fifty books from the main shop were damaged.

Emma's clothes were **soaked**. Her hair was wet. Her hands were cold. But she kept working. Everyone did.

The power went out at noon. The whole street was dark. Mrs. Penny brought over a box of supplies from her tea shop. She lit a **candle** and placed it on the counter. Then another, and another. Soon the bookshop was full of soft, warm light.

They sat on the floor among the books and drank tea from a flask. Nobody spoke for a while. They were tired. But they were together.

---

### Passage 4
**Vocab used: `shelter`, `relief`, `grateful`**

The bookshop became a kind of **shelter** that day. People came and went. Some brought food. Others brought dry towels. The teenager, whose name was Sam, brought his dog, who sat quietly in the corner.

By late afternoon, the rain stopped. The wind calmed down. Sunlight broke through the clouds. Emma felt a wave of **relief** wash over her. The worst was over.

She looked around the shop. It was a mess — wet towels, buckets, books spread across every surface. But the important thing was that most of the books were safe. The people had saved them.

Emma felt deeply **grateful**. These people did not know her well. She had been in Saltwick for only a few weeks. But they had come without being asked. They had worked for hours. They had treated the bookshop like it was their own.

She understood something then. This bookshop did not just belong to her. It belonged to the whole town.

---

### Passage 5
**Vocab used: `celebrate`, `repair`, `strength`**

That evening, Mrs. Penny opened her tea shop for everyone. "We need to **celebrate**," she said. "We survived the storm!"

She made pots of tea and brought out cakes she had been saving. People laughed and talked. Tom sat next to Emma and smiled. "The roof will need a proper **repair**," he said. "I can do it next week."

"Thank you," Emma said. "For everything."

Jack raised his cup. "To Margaret's bookshop," he said. "And to Emma, who is keeping it alive."

Everyone raised their cups. Emma felt her eyes sting with tears, but she smiled.

She thought about the **strength** of this community. They were not rich. They were not powerful. But they were kind, and they showed up when it mattered. That was worth more than any salary or promotion.

That night, Emma made her decision. She would not sell the bookshop. She would stay. She would make it work. She did not know how yet, but she knew she was not alone.

## Chapter 8: Những Lá Thư (The Letters)

### Vocabulary List
`letter`, `regret`, `argument`, `forgive`, `emotion`, `envelope`, `handwriting`, `apology`, `relationship`, `distance`, `understand`, `tears`, `comfort`, `heal`, `courage`

---

### Passage 1
**Vocab used: `letter`, `envelope`, `handwriting`**

The next day, Emma started cleaning the bookshop properly. Tom had covered the damaged part of the roof with a temporary sheet. The floor was still damp in places, but the books were safe.

While moving a heavy shelf in the back room, Emma found a wooden box behind it. It was old and dusty. She opened it carefully.

Inside were dozens of **letters**. Each one was in a cream-coloured **envelope**, sealed but never posted. There were no stamps on them. They had never been sent.

Emma picked one up. She recognized the **handwriting** on the front immediately. It was Gran's — the same neat, small letters she had seen in the book notes.

The name on the envelope made Emma's heart stop. It said: "Sarah Clarke." That was Emma's mother.

Emma sat down on the floor. She held the envelope in both hands. Her mother had left Saltwick when Emma was a baby. She never came back. She never talked about Gran or the town. Emma had always wondered why.

---

### Passage 2
**Vocab used: `regret`, `argument`, `apology`**

Emma opened the first letter. It was dated fifteen years ago.

"Dear Sarah," it began. "I think about you every day. I know you are angry with me. I have so much **regret** about what happened."

Emma read on. Gran wrote about an **argument** they had. It was about Emma's father. Gran had not approved of him. She had said things she should not have said. Sarah had packed her bags and left with baby Emma. She never came back.

"I was wrong," Gran wrote. "I should not have said those things. I am sorry. This is my **apology**, even if you never read it."

Emma's hands were shaking. She had never known any of this. Her mother had told her almost nothing about Gran or Saltwick. Now she understood why.

She opened another letter. And another. Each one was full of love and sadness. Gran had written to Sarah for years, but she had never posted the letters. She had kept them all in this box, hidden behind the shelf.

---

### Passage 3
**Vocab used: `emotion`, `relationship`, `distance`**

The letters were full of **emotion**. Some were long — three or four pages. Others were short, just a few lines. But every one of them said the same thing: I miss you. I love you. Please come home.

Gran wrote about the bookshop, about the town, about the seasons changing. She wrote about Emma too. "I hope Emma is growing up happy. I hope she likes books. I hope she knows I love her."

Emma learned about the **relationship** between Gran and her mother. It had been close once. Sarah had grown up in the bookshop. She had loved it. But the argument broke something between them.

The **distance** between them grew over the years. Gran kept writing, but Sarah never replied — because she never received the letters. Gran was too afraid to send them. Too afraid of being rejected again.

Emma put the letters down. She needed air. She walked outside and stood in the street, breathing deeply. The sea air was cold and clean.

---

### Passage 4
**Vocab used: `understand`, `tears`, `comfort`**

Emma went back inside and read more letters. She began to **understand** why Gran had kept the bookshop all these years. It was not just about books. It was about connection. It was Gran's way of keeping a door open — hoping that one day, Sarah would walk through it.

The bookshop was Gran's love letter to her daughter. Every book on the shelf, every note inside a cover, every cup of tea with a customer — it was all part of the same thing. Gran was saying: I am still here. I am waiting.

**Tears** ran down Emma's face. She did not try to stop them. She cried for Gran, for her mother, for all the lost years.

Tom found her sitting on the floor of the back room, surrounded by letters. He did not ask questions. He sat down next to her and put his hand on her shoulder. The **comfort** of his presence was enough. She did not need words. She just needed someone to be there.

---

### Passage 5
**Vocab used: `forgive`, `heal`, `courage`**

That evening, Emma sat in Gran's chair and thought about everything she had learned. Gran had made a mistake. She had said hurtful things. But she had spent the rest of her life trying to make it right.

Could Emma's mother **forgive** Gran? Could she forgive herself for staying away so long? Emma did not know. But she knew that the letters deserved to be read. Her mother deserved to know how much Gran had loved her.

Some wounds take a long time to **heal**. Emma understood that now. But healing could not begin without honesty. Without reaching out. Without taking the first step.

She would need **courage** to contact her mother. They had not spoken in months. Their own relationship was not easy. But Emma had the letters now. She had the truth. And she had a reason to try.

She put the letters back in the box carefully. She would keep them safe. And when the time was right, she would share them.

## Chapter 9: Khai Trương Lại (The Reopening)

### Vocabulary List
`reopen`, `decorate`, `invite`, `event`, `speech`, `audience`, `applause`, `proud`, `display`, `arrange`, `banner`, `crowd`, `atmosphere`, `success`, `belong`

---

### Passage 1
**Vocab used: `reopen`, `decorate`, `banner`**

Two weeks later, the roof was fixed. Tom had done a beautiful job. The bookshop was dry, clean, and ready.

Emma decided to **reopen** the shop properly. Not just open the door quietly — she wanted to make it special. A fresh start.

She and Mrs. Penny spent three days getting ready. They cleaned every shelf. They sorted the books by category. They put new price labels on everything.

Mrs. Penny brought flowers from her garden. Tom built a new display table from old wood. Emma painted the front door a brighter shade of green.

They decided to **decorate** the shop for the opening day. Mrs. Penny made bunting from old fabric. Sam, the teenager from the storm, helped hang it across the ceiling.

Tom made a large wooden **banner** for the front of the shop. It said: "Clarke's Books — Open Again." The letters were carved by hand. Emma touched them gently. It was perfect.

---

### Passage 2
**Vocab used: `invite`, `event`, `arrange`**

Emma wanted the whole town to come. She wrote a simple notice and put copies in every shop on the street. She asked Mrs. Penny to **invite** her regular customers too.

"This is going to be a real **event**," Mrs. Penny said happily. She was already planning the food. "I'll make sandwiches and cakes. And tea, of course. Lots of tea."

Tom helped Emma **arrange** the inside of the shop. They moved the shelves to create more space. They set up a table near the door for food and drinks. They put Gran's favourite chair in a special spot by the window.

Emma also prepared the rare book room. She did not want to hide it anymore. She cleaned the shelves, added small labels to each book, and opened the door wide. It would become a reading room — a quiet place where anyone could sit and read.

Jack came by to see the preparations. He stood in the doorway and smiled. "Margaret would love this," he said.

---

### Passage 3
**Vocab used: `crowd`, `atmosphere`, `speech`**

Saturday morning arrived. The sun was shining. Emma opened the door at ten o'clock.

By half past ten, the shop was full. A **crowd** of people filled the small space — families, couples, children, elderly neighbours. Some Emma recognized from the storm. Others she had never seen before.

The **atmosphere** was warm and happy. People talked, laughed, and looked at the books. Children sat on the floor and read picture books. Mrs. Penny served tea and cake from behind a small table.

At eleven o'clock, Mrs. Penny tapped a spoon against a cup. "Emma has something to say," she announced.

Emma stood near the front desk. She had not planned a long **speech**. She was nervous. But she looked at the faces around her and felt calm.

"Thank you all for coming," she began. "This bookshop was my grandmother's life. She built it for this town. She built it for all of you."

---

### Passage 4
**Vocab used: `audience`, `applause`, `proud`**

The **audience** was quiet. Everyone listened. Emma could see Mrs. Penny wiping her eyes. Jack stood at the back, his arms crossed, nodding slowly.

"I came to Saltwick planning to sell this place," Emma continued. "But you showed me what it means. During the storm, you came to save the books. You didn't do it for me. You did it because this shop matters to you."

She paused. "So I'm staying. I'm going to keep the bookshop open. For Gran. For all of you. And for me."

The room erupted in **applause**. People clapped and cheered. Mrs. Penny hugged Emma tightly. Tom smiled from across the room.

Jack walked forward. He was carrying a bunch of wildflowers. He gave them to Emma. "Your gran would be very **proud** of you," he said quietly.

Emma held the flowers and looked around the room. She had never felt anything like this before. She had never felt so connected to a place and its people.

---

### Passage 5
**Vocab used: `display`, `success`, `belong`**

The rest of the day was wonderful. People bought books. Children drew pictures and pinned them to the wall. Tom set up a small **display** of local history books near the entrance.

The rare book room was popular. People walked in quietly and looked at the old books with wide eyes. Emma had put a sign on the door: "Gran's Library — Please sit and read."

By the end of the day, Emma had sold more books than she expected. But the **success** was not about money. It was about the feeling in the room. It was about people coming together around something they loved.

As the last customers left, Emma stood alone in the shop. The candles were still burning. The flowers were on the desk. The bunting hung from the ceiling.

She felt something she had not felt in a long time. She felt like she **belonged** here. Not because she had inherited the shop. But because she had chosen to stay. She had chosen this life, and it had chosen her back.

## Chapter 10: Nhà (Home)

### Vocabulary List
`settle`, `routine`, `peaceful`, `content`, `friendship`, `invite`, `harbour`, `reflect`, `grateful`, `chapter`, `beginning`, `promise`, `horizon`, `breathe`, `home`

---

### Passage 1
**Vocab used: `settle`, `routine`, `peaceful`**

Three months passed. Summer turned to autumn. The leaves on the trees changed from green to gold. The air grew cooler, and the sea turned a deeper shade of blue.

Emma had started to **settle** into her new life. She woke up early each morning, made tea, and walked to the bookshop. She opened the door at nine and closed it at five. She had found a small flat above the post office, just two minutes from the shop.

She had a **routine** now. Mornings were for organizing new books and writing orders. Afternoons were for customers. Evenings were for reading in Gran's chair by the window.

The town was **peaceful** in autumn. The summer tourists had gone. The streets were quiet again. Emma liked it this way. She liked the sound of the wind and the smell of the sea. She liked knowing the name of every person who walked through her door.

---

### Passage 2
**Vocab used: `content`, `friendship`, `invite`**

Emma was surprised by how **content** she felt. In London, she had always wanted more — a better job, a bigger flat, more money. Here, she wanted nothing. She had enough.

Her **friendship** with Tom had grown stronger. They had dinner together twice a week. He told her about the boats he was building. She told him about the books she was reading. They walked along the cliffs on Sunday mornings.

Mrs. Penny was like a second grandmother. She brought Emma soup when it was cold and flowers when it was sunny. She told stories about Gran that made Emma laugh and cry at the same time.

One evening, Emma sat down and wrote a letter to her mother. She told her about Saltwick, about the bookshop, about the people. She told her about Gran's letters — the ones that were never sent.

"I found them behind a shelf," Emma wrote. "They are full of love. I think you should read them. I want to **invite** you to come and visit. There is so much I want to show you."

---

### Passage 3
**Vocab used: `harbour`, `reflect`, `grateful`**

Emma posted the letter the next morning. She walked to the post office and put it in the box. Then she walked down to the **harbour** and sat on the wall.

The fishing boats were out. The water was silver in the morning light. A few seagulls flew in circles above her head.

She liked to come here to **reflect**. To think about how much had changed. Three months ago, she was sitting at a desk in London, tired and unhappy. Now she was here, running a bookshop in a town by the sea.

She felt **grateful** for so many things. For Gran, who had left her the shop. For Mrs. Penny, who had welcomed her on the first day. For Tom, who had fixed the window and the roof and had become her closest friend. For Jack, who had trusted her with Gran's secret.

She was grateful for the storm too. It had shown her what mattered. It had shown her that she was not alone.

---

### Passage 4
**Vocab used: `chapter`, `beginning`, `promise`**

Emma thought about her life as a book. London was one **chapter**. It was finished now. She had closed it gently and put it on the shelf.

Saltwick was a new **beginning**. A fresh page. She did not know how the story would end, but she was excited to find out. There would be hard days. There would be quiet days. But there would also be days like the reopening — full of warmth and light and laughter.

She thought about Gran. She wished she could talk to her. She wished she could say: I understand now. I understand why you stayed. I understand why you kept the bookshop. I understand why you wrote those letters.

Emma made a quiet **promise** to herself. She would take care of the bookshop. She would keep it open for the town. She would keep Gran's notes inside the books. And she would add her own.

She smiled. That felt right. That felt like something Gran would want.

---

### Passage 5
**Vocab used: `horizon`, `breathe`, `home`**

The sun was setting over the sea. The sky was painted in shades of orange, pink, and purple. The **horizon** was a thin golden line where the water met the sky.

Emma stood up from the harbour wall. She closed her eyes and let herself **breathe**. The salty air filled her lungs and made her feel alive.

She looked at the town behind her. The bookshop with its green door. Mrs. Penny's tea shop with the light on inside. The narrow streets and the old stone buildings. The cliffs rising white against the darkening sky.

This was **home**. Not because she was born here. Not because it was perfect. But because she had chosen it. She had found a place where she belonged, where people knew her name, where she could be herself.

She walked back up the street toward the bookshop. The door was closed, but the light was still on inside. She could see the shelves through the window, full of books and full of stories.

She put her hand on the door handle. It was warm from the last of the sun. She pushed it open and stepped inside.

She was home.
