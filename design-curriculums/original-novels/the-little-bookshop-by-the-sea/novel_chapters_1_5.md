# The Little Bookshop by the Sea — Chapters 1–5

---

## Chapter 1: Tin Buồn (The News)

### Vocabulary List
`lawyer`, `inherit`, `property`, `funeral`, `countryside`, `passenger`, `platform`, `luggage`, `journey`, `arrive`, `memory`, `stranger`, `nervous`, `envelope`, `signature`

---

### Passage 1
**Vocab used: `envelope`, `lawyer`, `signature`**

Emma sat at her kitchen table in London. It was a grey Tuesday morning. She was drinking tea and looking at her phone when the postman knocked.

He gave her a large brown **envelope**. It looked official. Her name was typed on the front: Emma Clarke.

She opened it carefully. Inside was a letter from a **lawyer** named Mr. Davies. The letter said that her grandmother, Margaret Clarke, had died two weeks ago. Emma read the words again slowly. She did not cry. She just sat very still.

The letter asked her to come to Cornwall. There were papers to sign. At the bottom of the page, there was a line for her **signature**. She put the letter down and looked out the window. The sky was the same grey as before, but everything felt different now.

---

### Passage 2
**Vocab used: `inherit`, `property`, `funeral`**

The letter explained more. Emma's grandmother had left her something. She would **inherit** a small bookshop in a town called Saltwick. Emma had not been there since she was eight years old.

The bookshop was the only **property** Gran owned. It was on the main street, near the sea. Mr. Davies wrote that the shop had been closed since Gran became ill.

The **funeral** had already happened. Emma felt a sharp pain in her chest when she read that. Nobody had told her. She had not spoken to Gran in years. Her mother never talked about Cornwall or the family there. Now it was too late.

Emma put the letter back in the envelope. She needed to go. She did not know what she would do with a bookshop, but she knew she had to see it.

---

### Passage 3
**Vocab used: `countryside`, `passenger`, `platform`**

The next morning, Emma took a taxi to Paddington Station. She bought a ticket to Cornwall. The train would take five hours.

The **countryside** outside London was flat and green. Emma watched it pass through the window. Fields, farms, small villages. Everything moved slowly here. It was so different from the city.

She was the only **passenger** in her section of the train. It was quiet. She could hear the sound of the wheels on the track. She closed her eyes for a moment and tried to remember Gran's face.

At the last stop, she stood on the **platform** with her bag. The air smelled like salt and grass. A small sign said "Saltwick — 3 miles." There were no taxis. She would have to find another way.

---

### Passage 4
**Vocab used: `luggage`, `journey`, `arrive`**

Emma pulled her **luggage** behind her. She had packed only one small suitcase. She did not plan to stay long — maybe three or four days. Just enough time to sign the papers and decide what to do with the shop.

The **journey** from the station to the town was along a narrow road. She walked past hedges and stone walls. A few cars passed, but mostly it was just her and the birds.

After forty minutes, she began to see houses. Then a church. Then a row of shops. She had started to **arrive**. The town was small and quiet. The buildings were old, painted white and blue. She could hear the sea somewhere close.

Emma stopped walking. She looked around. She was here. She was really here.

---

### Passage 5
**Vocab used: `memory`, `stranger`, `nervous`**

Standing in the middle of the main street, Emma felt a **memory** come back. She was eight years old, holding Gran's hand, walking to the bookshop. Gran was laughing. The sun was warm. That was the last summer she visited.

Now she was a **stranger** here. Nobody knew her. The few people on the street looked at her with polite curiosity but did not stop. She was just a woman with a suitcase.

She felt **nervous**. What would the bookshop look like? Would it be empty? Would it feel like Gran? She did not know what to expect.

She took a deep breath and started walking again. The bookshop was somewhere on this street. She would find it. She had come this far. There was no reason to stop now.

## Chapter 2: Thị Trấn Ven Biển (The Seaside Town)

### Vocabulary List
`harbour`, `cliff`, `seagull`, `cobblestone`, `narrow`, `dusty`, `shelf`, `counter`, `welcome`, `neighbour`, `cheerful`, `kettle`, `biscuit`, `sunset`, `peaceful`

---

### Passage 1
**Vocab used: `harbour`, `cliff`, `seagull`**

Saltwick was a small town built around a **harbour**. Fishing boats sat in the water, painted red and blue. The sea was calm and grey-green. Emma could smell the salt in the air.

Behind the town, tall white **cliffs** rose up against the sky. They looked like walls made of chalk. Emma had never seen anything like them — not in real life, only in pictures.

A **seagull** cried above her head. Then another. They flew in circles over the harbour, looking for food. The sound was loud and wild. It made Emma feel far away from London, far away from her flat, her job, her normal life.

She walked along the harbour wall for a few minutes. The town was beautiful. It was also very quiet. There were no crowds, no traffic, no noise. Just the sea, the birds, and the wind.

---

### Passage 2
**Vocab used: `cobblestone`, `narrow`, `dusty`**

Emma turned onto the main street. The road was made of old **cobblestones**, smooth and uneven. Her suitcase wheels bumped over them noisily.

The street was **narrow**. Small shops stood on both sides — a bakery, a post office, a charity shop. Most of them were closed. It was late afternoon, and the town seemed to be resting.

Then she saw it. A shop with a dark green door and a wooden sign: "Clarke's Books." The windows were **dusty**. Emma could not see inside clearly. There was a "Closed" sign hanging on the door.

She stood in front of it for a long time. This was Gran's bookshop. This was the place she had inherited. It looked old and tired, but there was something about it — something warm. She reached out and touched the door handle. It was cold.

---

### Passage 3
**Vocab used: `shelf`, `counter`, `welcome`**

Before Emma could try the door, she heard a voice behind her.

"You must be Emma!"

She turned around. A woman with white hair and a bright smile was standing outside the shop next door — a tea shop called "Penny's."

"I'm Mrs. Penny. Your gran talked about you all the time. Come in, come in!"

Mrs. Penny did not wait for an answer. She took Emma's arm and led her inside the tea shop. It was small and warm. There was a long wooden **counter** with cakes under glass. Every **shelf** on the wall held teapots and cups of different colours.

"Sit down, dear. You are very **welcome** here," Mrs. Penny said. "Everyone in this town loved your gran. She was a wonderful woman."

Emma sat down. She did not know what to say. She had not expected anyone to know who she was.

---

### Passage 4
**Vocab used: `neighbour`, `cheerful`, `kettle`**

Mrs. Penny moved quickly around the tea shop. She was a good **neighbour** — the kind who always had time to talk and always had food to share.

"Your gran and I were friends for thirty years," she said. Her voice was **cheerful**, even when she talked about sad things. "She came here every morning for tea. Every single morning. Rain or sun."

She put the **kettle** on and took out two cups. "She talked about you, you know. She always hoped you would come back one day."

Emma felt her throat tighten. "I didn't know she was ill," she said quietly.

"She didn't want anyone to worry," Mrs. Penny said. "That was Margaret. Strong until the end."

The kettle boiled. Mrs. Penny poured the tea and sat down across from Emma. She looked at her with kind eyes. "So. What are you going to do with the bookshop?"

"I don't know yet," Emma said honestly.

---

### Passage 5
**Vocab used: `biscuit`, `sunset`, `peaceful`**

Mrs. Penny pushed a plate of **biscuits** toward Emma. "Eat. You've had a long day."

Emma took one. It was buttery and sweet. She realized she had not eaten since morning.

They talked for a long time. Mrs. Penny told her about the town, about the people, about Gran's life here. The bookshop had been open for forty years. People came from other towns to visit it. Gran knew every customer by name.

When Emma finally left the tea shop, the sky was orange and pink. The **sunset** over the harbour was beautiful. She stood and watched it for a moment.

The town was **peaceful**. There were no sirens, no buses, no rush. Just the sound of the waves and the last light of the day.

Emma looked at the bookshop door again. Tomorrow, she would go inside. Tonight, she would rest. Mrs. Penny had given her the key and the address of a small bed and breakfast down the street.

She picked up her suitcase and walked slowly toward it.

## Chapter 3: Tiệm Sách (The Bookshop)

### Vocabulary List
`receipt`, `organize`, `pile`, `discover`, `handwriting`, `message`, `customer`, `recommend`, `dust`, `wooden`, `ladder`, `ceiling`, `curious`, `collection`, `treasure`

---

### Passage 1
**Vocab used: `dust`, `pile`, `wooden`**

The next morning, Emma opened the bookshop door. The air inside was thick with **dust**. She coughed and waved her hand in front of her face.

Books were everywhere. There were **piles** of them on the floor, on chairs, on the stairs. Some were stacked so high they looked like they might fall at any moment.

The floor was **wooden** and old. It creaked under her feet as she walked in. The walls were covered with shelves, and every shelf was full. There were novels, cookbooks, children's books, history books. Hundreds of them.

Emma looked around slowly. The shop was a mess. It had clearly been closed for months. Nobody had cleaned or tidied anything. But even in this state, she could feel something. The shop had character. It had life. It was not just a room full of books. It was a place where something had happened.

---

### Passage 2
**Vocab used: `receipt`, `organize`, `ladder`**

Emma found the front desk. There was an old cash register and a box full of paper **receipts**. She picked one up. It was from two years ago. Gran had written the title of the book and the price by hand.

She decided to start cleaning. She needed to **organize** this place before she could think about anything else. She found a broom in the back room and began sweeping.

There was a tall **ladder** leaning against one of the bookshelves. Emma climbed it carefully. The top shelves were full of old books with leather covers. Some of them looked very old.

She worked for two hours. She swept the floor, opened the windows, and let fresh air in. The shop began to look better. Not perfect, but better. She could see the floor now. She could walk without stepping on books.

---

### Passage 3
**Vocab used: `discover`, `handwriting`, `message`**

While cleaning, Emma began to **discover** something interesting. Inside many of the books, there were small pieces of paper. Notes. Written in blue ink.

She recognized the **handwriting** immediately. It was Gran's. Neat, small letters that leaned slightly to the right. Emma had seen it on birthday cards when she was a child.

Each note was a short **message**. Some were about the book: "This one will make you cry — in a good way." Others were more personal: "For anyone who needs a friend today." One said simply: "Read this on a rainy afternoon."

Emma sat on the floor and read note after note. Gran had written hundreds of them. She had put them inside books like little gifts, waiting for someone to find them.

Emma's eyes were wet. She wiped them with her sleeve. She had not expected this. She had not expected to feel so close to someone she had not seen in seventeen years.

---

### Passage 4
**Vocab used: `customer`, `recommend`, `ceiling`**

Emma kept reading the notes. Some of them were written to specific people.

"For Mrs. Penny — because she loves a good love story."
"For Tom — something about the sea."
"For Jack — he'll understand this one."

Gran had known every **customer**. She did not just sell books. She would **recommend** the right book for the right person. She matched people with stories. That was her gift.

Emma looked up at the **ceiling**. It was high and painted white, but the paint was cracking. A small window near the top let in a square of sunlight. Dust floated in the light like tiny stars.

This bookshop was more than a business. It was a place of connection. Gran had built something special here. Emma was only beginning to understand what it was.

---

### Passage 5
**Vocab used: `curious`, `collection`, `treasure`**

Emma was **curious** now. She wanted to know more. She opened more books, found more notes. Each one told her something new about Gran — her humour, her kindness, her love of stories.

In the back room, she found a **collection** of Gran's favourite books on a small shelf. They were marked with a gold star on the spine. There were about twenty of them. Emma picked one up and opened it. Inside, Gran had written: "This book changed my life."

Emma held it to her chest. This whole shop was a **treasure**. Not because of money, but because of what it meant. Every book, every note, every shelf told a story — not just the story in the pages, but the story of Gran's life.

She sat in Gran's old chair behind the desk. It was soft and worn. She looked at the shop around her. For the first time since she arrived, she did not think about selling. She just thought about staying a little longer.

## Chapter 4: Người Thợ Mộc (The Carpenter)

### Vocabulary List
`carpenter`, `repair`, `window`, `tool`, `conversation`, `community`, `important`, `comfortable`, `regular`, `lock`, `key`, `basement`, `mystery`, `wonder`, `trust`

---

### Passage 1
**Vocab used: `carpenter`, `repair`, `window`**

On her third morning in Saltwick, Emma heard a knock on the bookshop door. She opened it and found a tall man with brown hair and kind eyes. He was carrying a bag.

"Hi. I'm Tom," he said. "I'm a **carpenter**. Mrs. Penny told me you were here. I've come to **repair** the broken window."

Emma looked at the side **window**. She had noticed the crack yesterday but had not thought about fixing it yet.

"Gran asked me to fix it months ago," Tom said. "I never got the chance before she..." He stopped. "Anyway. I'd like to do it now, if that's okay."

"Of course," Emma said. "Thank you."

Tom smiled and walked to the window. He moved quietly and carefully. Emma watched him for a moment, then went back to cleaning. It was nice to have someone else in the shop. It made the place feel less empty.

---

### Passage 2
**Vocab used: `tool`, `conversation`, `community`**

Tom worked slowly and carefully. He took each **tool** from his bag one at a time — a hammer, a measuring tape, a small saw. He seemed to know exactly what he was doing.

After a while, they started a **conversation**. Tom asked Emma about London. She asked him about Saltwick. He had lived here his whole life. He loved the sea, the quiet, the slow pace.

"This bookshop is important to the **community**," Tom said, measuring the glass. "People don't just come here to buy books. They come to sit, to talk, to feel like they belong somewhere."

Emma listened carefully. She had not thought about the shop that way. To her, it was just a building with books. But to the people here, it was something more.

"Your gran understood that," Tom said. "She made everyone feel welcome. Even people who never bought a single book."

---

### Passage 3
**Vocab used: `important`, `comfortable`, `regular`**

Tom told Emma more about the bookshop. It was **important** to the town in ways she had not imagined. On rainy days, people came inside just to sit and read. Gran always had tea ready.

"There was a **comfortable** chair by the window," Tom said. "Old Mr. Harris sat there every Wednesday. He never bought anything. He just read the newspaper and talked to your gran."

"Was he a **regular**?" Emma asked.

"Everyone was a regular," Tom said with a small laugh. "This town is small. Your gran knew everyone. And everyone knew her."

Emma thought about her life in London. She worked in a big office. She knew her colleagues' names, but not much else. She ate lunch alone most days. She had friends, but she was always busy.

Here, things were different. People knew each other. They cared about each other. The bookshop was at the centre of that.

---

### Passage 4
**Vocab used: `lock`, `key`, `basement`**

After Tom finished the window, Emma made tea. They sat at the front desk and drank it together. Tom looked around the shop with a warm expression.

"Have you been to the back room?" he asked.

"Yes," Emma said. "I cleaned it yesterday."

"What about the door at the end of the hall? The one with the **lock**?"

Emma frowned. "I saw it. I tried to open it, but I don't have the **key**."

Tom nodded slowly. "That room has been locked for years. Your gran never let anyone in there. Not even Mrs. Penny."

"What's in there?" Emma asked.

"I don't know. Maybe a **basement** storage room? Maybe something else." Tom shrugged. "Your gran had her reasons."

Emma felt a spark of curiosity. A locked room in the back of the bookshop. What was Gran hiding?

---

### Passage 5
**Vocab used: `mystery`, `wonder`, `trust`**

The locked room was a **mystery**. Emma thought about it all afternoon. She looked at the door again. It was heavy and old, made of dark wood. The lock was brass and looked expensive.

She began to **wonder** about Gran's life. There was so much she did not know. Gran had lived here for forty years. She had run this bookshop, made friends, built a life. And she had kept secrets.

Emma thought about Tom. He seemed honest and kind. She felt she could **trust** him. He had known Gran. He cared about the bookshop. He was not trying to sell her anything or push her to make a decision.

That evening, Emma sat in Gran's chair and looked at the locked door from across the room. She would find out what was behind it. But first, she needed to talk to the people who knew Gran best.

Tomorrow, she would ask Mrs. Penny. And she would find Jack — Gran's oldest friend.

## Chapter 5: Bí Mật Của Bà (Gran's Secret)

### Vocabulary List
`secret`, `valuable`, `rare`, `ancient`, `protect`, `hesitate`, `honest`, `reveal`, `shelf`, `leather`, `fragile`, `worth`, `fortune`, `promise`, `guard`

---

### Passage 1
**Vocab used: `secret`, `hesitate`, `honest`**

The next morning, Emma went to Penny's tea shop. She sat down and asked directly: "Mrs. Penny, do you know about the locked room in the bookshop?"

Mrs. Penny's smile faded. She put down her teacup slowly. "Your gran had her **secrets**, dear."

"But do you know what's in there?"

Mrs. Penny did not answer right away. She seemed to **hesitate**. She looked at the table, then at Emma, then at the table again.

"I think you should talk to Jack," she said finally. "He knew your gran better than anyone. He'll be **honest** with you."

"Who is Jack?" Emma asked.

"Jack Tremayne. He's a retired fisherman. He lives in the blue house by the harbour. He and your gran were friends for fifty years."

Emma thanked Mrs. Penny and left. She walked quickly toward the harbour. She needed answers.

---

### Passage 2
**Vocab used: `reveal`, `valuable`, `rare`**

Jack was sitting outside his house, looking at the sea. He was an old man with white hair and deep lines on his face. His eyes were sharp and blue.

"You're Margaret's granddaughter," he said before Emma could speak. "You look like her."

Emma sat down next to him. "I need to know about the locked room in the bookshop."

Jack was quiet for a long time. Then he spoke. "Your gran asked me never to **reveal** this. But she's gone now. And you have a right to know."

He told her the truth. Behind the locked door, Gran kept a collection of **valuable** books. They were **rare** — first editions, signed copies, books that were hundreds of years old.

"She collected them her whole life," Jack said. "She found them at markets, in old houses, at auctions. She had an eye for it."

Emma listened, her heart beating fast.

---

### Passage 3
**Vocab used: `protect`, `ancient`, `fortune`**

"Why did she hide them?" Emma asked.

"To **protect** them," Jack said simply. "Some of those books are very old. **Ancient**, even. They need special care — the right temperature, the right light. She didn't want people touching them or trying to buy them."

"Are they worth a lot of money?"

Jack looked at her carefully. "Some of them are worth a small **fortune**. But that's not why she kept them. She loved books. She believed they were important — not as objects to sell, but as things to preserve."

Emma thought about this. Gran had spent her life surrounded by books. She sold the ordinary ones and protected the special ones. It made sense. It was exactly the kind of thing Gran would do.

"She never told anyone?" Emma asked.

"She told me," Jack said. "And now I'm telling you."

---

### Passage 4
**Vocab used: `promise`, `guard`, `leather`**

Jack reached into his pocket and took out a small brass key. It was old and heavy.

"She gave me this ten years ago," he said. "She made me **promise** to give it to the right person at the right time."

"And I'm the right person?" Emma asked.

"You're her granddaughter. The shop is yours now. The books are yours too." Jack put the key in Emma's hand. "She asked me to **guard** this key. I've kept it safe all these years."

Emma looked at the key. It was warm from Jack's pocket. She closed her fingers around it.

"The books are in good condition," Jack said. "She kept them in **leather** cases. She checked on them every week. Even when she was ill, she went into that room."

Emma stood up. "Thank you, Jack."

He nodded. "Go and see them. You'll understand."

---

### Passage 5
**Vocab used: `shelf`, `fragile`, `worth`**

Emma walked back to the bookshop. Her hands were shaking slightly. She went to the locked door and put the key in. It turned smoothly.

The door opened into a small, cool room. Emma stepped inside and stopped.

Every **shelf** was full of beautiful books. They were arranged carefully by size and colour. Some had gold letters on the spine. Some were very small. Some were very large.

She picked up one book carefully. It was **fragile** — the pages were thin and yellow. The cover was soft leather with a title in Latin. She opened it gently. Inside, Gran had written: "Found in a farmhouse in Devon, 1987."

Emma looked around the room. There were maybe two hundred books here. She did not know exactly what they were all **worth**, but she could feel their importance. This was Gran's life's work. Her private collection. Her love, preserved on these shelves.

Emma sat on the floor of the small room and cried. Not because she was sad. Because she finally understood.
