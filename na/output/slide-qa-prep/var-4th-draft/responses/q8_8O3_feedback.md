# Evaluation of Presenter's Response

## Overall Assessment
The presenter provides a reasonable directional answer but misses a significant opportunity to leverage the rich data on the slides to deliver a more compelling, evidence-based response. The answer feels improvised rather than grounded in the prepared materials.

---

## 1. Accuracy — ⚠️ Partially Aligned

**What aligns well:**
- The presenter correctly acknowledges that the threshold needs recalibration and that work is underway to propose a new one.
- References to P13 improvement are accurate — the slides show P13 improved from ~52% (2023) to 84.3% (2025).
- The mention of lower business volume is consistent with the data (FYP declining from $222M in 2020 to $53M in 2025).
- The point about new compensation schemes potentially increasing VaR is a legitimate forward-looking consideration.

**Accuracy concerns:**
- The presenter says the new compensation scheme is "effective from January 2026" — this cannot be verified from the slides and introduces unsubstantiated claims.
- The presenter mentions a "new ILP product launching from March" — again, not on the slides. While this may be true business context, it introduces information the audience cannot verify against the presented materials.
- The presenter says these factors "might definitely going to increase VaR" — this is internally contradictory language ("might" vs. "definitely") and speculative without quantification.
- The presenter never references the actual current VaR figure of **$0.29 million** or the **0.6% VaR/FYP ratio**, which are prominently displayed on the slides.

---

## 2. Completeness — ❌ Significant Gaps

**Key points the presenter missed:**

1. **The strongest defense was sitting right on the slides.** The data shows VaR/FYP at 0.6% — comparable to the 0.8% levels in 2020-2021 when the business was 4x larger. This demonstrates the threshold was originally set for a fundamentally different business scale, and the presenter should have made this point explicitly.

2. **The $3M threshold was likely set during the 2022-2023 spike period** when VaR peaked at $3.8M. The presenter should have contextualized *why* the threshold was set at $3M and why it made sense then but needs updating now.

3. **The granular risk decomposition was completely ignored.** The slides meticulously show that:
   - 87% of units with positive VaR have less than $2K exposure
   - 95% are non-MBA Pro (not high-risk profiles)
   - 63% are non-GTF
   - Only 5 units warrant deep-dive attention
   - $0.05M of the $0.29M is from calculation-gap cases, not actual risk

   This data powerfully supports the argument that the current risk profile is fundamentally benign, and a recalibrated threshold should reflect this.

4. **The monitoring framework was not mentioned.** The presenter could have reassured the questioner that regardless of where the threshold lands, monthly monitoring, escalation protocols, and multi-indicator screening are already in place — meaning a lower threshold would be operationally manageable.

5. **No specific timeline or process was offered** for when the new threshold proposal would be ready, despite the questioner clearly expecting accountability on this point.

---

## 3. Clarity — ❌ Needs Significant Improvement

**Structural issues:**
- The response lacks a clear framework. It meanders between acknowledging the problem, discussing factors that reduce VaR, and then pivoting to factors that increase VaR, without a clear organizing principle.
- The answer ends with "we'll update you along the process" — which is vague and may leave the audience feeling the question was deflected rather than answered.

**Language issues:**
- "Intuitively, we think" — this undermines credibility in a risk governance setting. The audience wants data-driven conclusions, not intuition.
- "Might definitely going to increase" — contradictory and grammatically unclear.
- "It's going to be a hook in the market" — informal/colloquial language inappropriate for a risk committee audience.
- Multiple hedging phrases ("might also," "could also might") create an impression of uncertainty rather than measured analysis.

---

## Suggested Improved Response

> "That's an excellent question, and the short answer is **yes — we agree the $3 million threshold needs recalibration**, and we are actively working on a proposal.
>
> Let me ground this in the data. The $3 million threshold was appropriate when our business was generating $200+ million in FYP with 50,000-60,000 agents. Today, our FYP is $53 million — roughly a quarter of that scale — with agent count reduced to about 13,600. Our current VaR of $0.29 million is less than 10% of the threshold, and importantly, our **VaR-to-FYP ratio of 0.6% is actually in line with the healthy 2020-2021 period** when the business was much larger.
>
> Furthermore, when we decompose the $0.29 million, **$0.05 million is from calculation-gap cases** — timing differences and indirect team production mismatches — not actual risk. Of the 260 units with positive VaR, **87% have exposure below $2,000 annually**, which is not meaningful enough to incentivize arbitrage behavior. Only 5 units warrant close monitoring, and all are already under our monthly deep-dive review.
>
> Now, in calibrating the new threshold, we are accounting for **two upward pressures on VaR in 2026**: first, our revised compensation scheme effective January 2026 with enhanced bonus rates for top UMs, particularly MBA Pro; and second, a new ILP product launching in March with higher case sizes that we expect will drive significant sales growth. Both could mechanically increase VaR without necessarily increasing risk.
>
> Our approach is to **quantify the expected VaR impact of these two factors**, separate structural risk from business-growth-driven VaR increases, and propose a threshold that is appropriately calibrated to our current and projected business scale. We expect to present this proposal by [specific timeline]. In the meantime, our full monthly monitoring framework — including multi-indicator screening and escalation protocols — remains fully operational regardless of where the formal threshold sits."
