## PART 1: Intro & Market Overview
**Speaker: Tran Huynh Duc (PD) — Slides 1–10 — ~1.5 minutes**

**[Slide 1 — Title]**

Good afternoon everyone. We are the "Simple Insurance Knowledge" team — Hien from Marketing, Tien from Agency, Thanh from IT, and myself, Duc, from Product Development.

Our mission is simple: take insurance from complexity to clarity.

**[Slide 2 — The Objective]**

Our objective is to reframe how life insurance is understood — by simplifying its explanation so customers can clearly see its role in long-term protection, financial security, and legacy planning.

Important note: this is purely about education and communication. We are not changing product design or operations.

**[Slide 3 — Table of Content]**

We'll walk you through five sections today: Market Overview, Competitor Overview, our Approach, the Implementation, and finally Timeline and Budget.

Let's start with the market.

**[Slide 4 — Section Divider: Market Overview]**

*(pause briefly)*

**[Slide 5 — Market Data]**

A 2023 survey by Vietnam Report revealed something alarming: nearly 60% of policyholders did not truly understand the insurance product they had purchased. For investment-linked products, that number jumps to 98%.

This is not a minor gap — this is a systemic failure in how insurance is communicated.

**[Slide 6 — Contract Complexity]**

And it's not just customers saying this. Members of the National Assembly have publicly stated that insurance contracts are — and I quote — "impossibly long and difficult to understand." Even lawmakers with legal expertise admitted they struggle to read through them.

**[Slide 7 — Insurance and I are like exes]**

There's a popular saying online: "Insurance and I are like exes — we've crossed paths, but we never really got each other." That captures the public sentiment perfectly. The trust gap is real, and it's widening.

**[Slide 8 — Patience Test]**

Here's another one: "Need a patience test? Try finishing an insurance sales illustration." These aren't just jokes — they reflect genuine frustration from real customers.

**[Slide 9 — Challenge in Life]**

News articles continue to highlight cases where customers paid hundreds of millions of dong in premiums, only to receive a fraction back upon early termination — not because they were cheated, but because they never understood the fee structure in the first place.

**[Slide 10 — Core Problem]**

So what's the core problem? Complexity exists on two layers.

First, the product structure itself. Insurance documents follow legal logic — definitions, terms, exclusions, appendices. But customers think in a completely different way: "What do I get? When do I get paid? When am I not covered?" This mismatch forces customers to translate the content themselves.

Second, the communication layer. Advisor training focuses on sales skills and revenue KPIs. Clarity is never measured, so it never becomes a priority. The result? Customers receive incomplete or oversimplified explanations.

## PART 2: Competitive Overview
**Speaker: Ho Le Hieu Hien (MKT) — Slides 11–19 — ~1.5 minutes**

**[Slide 11 — Section Divider: Competitor Overview]**

Thank you, Duc. Now let me walk you through what our competitors are doing in the insurance education space.

**[Slide 12 — Prudential]**

Prudential focuses on educating users about insurance processes — payment, medical registration, and claims. They also run discussions about the role of life insurance and its financial protection function. Their approach uses minigames on social media to drive awareness and virality. Open-ended discussion topics help increase engagement.

**[Slide 13 — Dai-ichi Life]**

Dai-ichi Life takes a similar approach — general knowledge about health declarations, claim payments, and premium expectations. They combine educational content with product promotion through minigames and short-form TikTok videos. The content is relatable and attracts strong interest, but it also generates mixed and opposing opinions.

**[Slide 14 — Generali]**

Generali educates on insurance perceptions — payment principles, contract timelines, common misconceptions, and the purpose of insurance as protection. Their content attracted strong attention, but also sparked many opposing opinions in the comments.

**[Slide 15 — AIA]**

AIA runs a storytelling series called "Insurance is Protection," highlighting real-life accident cases and the role of insurance. Many comments praised the brand for its claim-handling cases. This approach helped highlight the role of insurance while positively strengthening the brand image.

**[Slide 16 — Sun Life]**

Sun Life has a similar storytelling series called "Lá Chắn" — or "The Shield" — covering real claiming cases, health protection, and investment planning. Most audience discussions were positive and supportive.

**[Slide 17–18 — FWD]**

FWD stands out with their "Bảo hiểm dễ hiểu" campaign — which translates to "Insurance Made Easy." They used celebrity influencers like Tóc Tiên and Đen Vâu to share personal perspectives on life planning and health protection. They also produced short educational videos explaining insurance in simple, relatable language. And critically, they built a dedicated education website that explains insurance concepts, benefits, and policy terms in plain language — reducing the complexity of insurance information.

**[Slide 19 — Key Points]**

So here are the key takeaways from the competitive landscape:

The industry is strong in emotional storytelling but weak in transparency. Trust now comes from understanding, not promises. The biggest risk is the gap between brand narrative and sales reality. Next-generation insurance needs to simplify the financial structure, not just the story. The market is shifting from category expansion to category correction.

**[Slide 23 — Where we want customers to be]**

Where do we want customers to be? Three things. First, faster understanding of the value of insurance. Second, better-informed health and wealth planning decisions. Third, stronger customer relationships built on clarity and trust.

**[Slide 24 — What needs to change]**

So what needs to change?

**[Slide 25 — How to achieve this]**

How might we achieve this outcome consistently and over time?

**[Slide 26 — Long-term platform]**

By building a long-term education platform — not a one-off campaign.

**[Slide 27 — Mission]**

Our mission: to translate complex insurance concepts into clear, consistent understanding that supports informed customer decisions.

**[Slide 28 — Equipping employees]**

We begin by equipping employees with simple, easy-to-understand product knowledge so they can confidently become Product Ambassadors.

**[Slide 29 — Project Narrative]**

We call this "The Insurance Translator" — translating insurance into the language of everyday life. Phase 1, our pilot, targets employees. Phase 2 expands to new agents. Phase 3 reaches customers directly.

**[Slide 30 — Do vs Don't]**

What we do: use clear, everyday language; explain how insurance supports long-term financial security; clarify how benefits work and what customers should expect; distinguish guaranteed versus non-guaranteed elements; and show how insurance fits within a broader financial plan.

What we do not touch: product structure, pricing, fund mechanics, projections, operations, claims, or system processes.

## PART 4: Demo — Product Explainer
**Speaker: Tran Ngoc Thanh (IT) — Slide 31–32 — ~2 minutes**

**[Slide 31 — Section Divider: Implementation]**

Now let me show you what we've actually built. The pilot phase translates product specifications and sales illustrations into everyday language.

**[Slide 32 — Product Explainer Demo]**

Our first demo is the Product Explainer for Xanh Tương Lai.

On the left, you see the traditional approach: 36 slides of dense legal and product text. No personalization. Same content for everyone. Staff skim it or skip it entirely. Low engagement, low retention.

On the right is our interactive explainer. Users enter their age, budget, family situation, and risk tolerance — and they get a personalized insurance story. They can explore interactive scenarios: what happens if the customer passes away, gets critically ill, or if the market crashes. Real-time calculations use actual product formulas. FAQ, glossary, fund information — all in one page.

Why does this matter? Staff don't need to memorize 36 slides. They experience the product through their own profile and see exactly how it works for a real customer. The interactive demo turns passive reading into active exploration.

## PART 5: Demo — Income Simulation
**Speaker: Tien Phan (Agency) — Slides 33–34 — ~2 minutes**

**[Slide 34 — Payout Simulator]**

Our second demo is the Insurance Payout Simulator.

Again, on the left: the traditional 36-page illustration document. Pages of tables, numbers, and legal disclaimers. Fixed to one customer profile. Staff struggle to explain payout mechanics. Customers are confused by a wall of numbers.

On the right: our interactive simulator. You can adjust age, premium, and fund allocation — and see results instantly. It tells a visual story: "What happens if you die in year 3? What about year 15? What if you get critically ill in year 30?" There's a year-by-year account value projection chart. Staff can demo this live to customers in meetings.

Why does this matter? A 36-page illustration document is a compliance artifact, not a learning tool. Staff can't internalize payout mechanics by reading tables — they need to play with the numbers. The simulator lets staff input any customer profile and instantly see how benefits work across scenarios. It builds confidence for real sales conversations.
