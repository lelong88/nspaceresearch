## PART 1: VaR Methodology (Slides 13–14)

**[Slide: Approved VaR Methodology]**

Good morning everyone. Let me walk you through our VaR review and 2026 threshold proposal.

First, a quick recap on methodology.

The formula is simple: **VaR = Total Payout minus Total FYP**.

Payout includes first-year commission, rider bonus, compensation, financing, contests, and benefits. We exclude CSV first-year — it's only about 0.4% of FYP due to high allocation charges, so it's negligible.

We measure on a rolling 12-month window. This captures the full sales cycle and smooths out timing effects from post-freelook payments.

We calculate at the direct team level for each UM+: total payout to the UM and their direct agents, minus FYP produced by that same direct team. Only teams with positive VaR count toward the agency total — negative VaR teams are zeroed out. We want to flag where payout exceeds production, not mask it behind profitable teams.

**[Slide: Agency Structure Illustration]**

This diagram shows how direct teams work. Each manager's VaR uses their income — which includes earnings from the whole team hierarchy, like overrides on indirect downlines — but only subtracts their direct team's FYP.

This gap between income scope and FYP scope is important. It explains many of the "calculation-gap" cases we'll discuss later. It's a feature of the formula, not a sign of risk.

**Why PSM+ is excluded from VaR measurement:**


**[Reference: Slide 15 — VaR by PSM+ Level]**

You may notice we don't include PSM+ in the VaR calculation. Let me explain why.

We reviewed VaR at every PSM+ reporting level — from CAO-1 all the way down to CAO-7 — across four years of data. The result: VaR is negative at every single PSM+ level, every single year. In 2021, CAO-1 level showed negative $70.6 million. In 2024, still negative $2.9 million. Even in 2023, our toughest year, VaR remained negative across all levels.

What does this tell us? At the PSM+ level, total FYP always exceeds total payout. There is no arbitrage exposure at that layer.

Arbitrage risk is more likely to happen — and is driven by — the FA and UM+ layers. That's where individual compensation can exceed individual production. So we measure and sum VaR at the UM+ unit level, which is where the actual risk sits.

That said, we don't ignore PSM+. The local compensation team monitors total PSM+ payout on a monthly basis and flags any significant fluctuations to Line1B for follow-up. And when violations are found at FA or UM+ level that connect to a PSM+, we can exercise clawback clauses, suspend campaign enrollment, or terminate contracts.

So the short answer: PSM+ VaR is consistently negative, measuring it would dilute the signal. We focus VaR where the risk actually lives — at the unit level — and monitor PSM+ through separate controls.

## PART 2: VaR Historical Review — Current Status (Slides 1–5)

**[Slide: 5-Year VaR Trend with Agency Performance Context]**

Now let's look at where we actually stand.

This slide shows our VaR trend over six years alongside agency performance. Let me walk through three distinct periods.

**2020–2022: The stable period.** VaR sat around $1.7 to $2.0 million. We were recruiting 50,000 to 60,000 new agents per year, had 6,000 to 9,000 UM+ leaders, and agency FYP was over $200 million annually. High production offset high payouts. VaR-to-FYP ratio stayed under 2%. This was a healthy, high-volume environment.

**2022–2023: The spike.** VaR peaked at $3.8 million at end of 2022, and VaR-to-FYP hit 4.0% in November 2023. What happened? Business volume collapsed — FYP dropped from $204 million to $72 million — due to multiple market headwinds. But compensation structures and commission rates stayed the same at around 32%. On top of that, deferred payments from the 2022 peak production year flowed into 2023. So you had high payouts meeting low production. That was the outlier.

**2024–2025: Where we are now.** VaR has been at $0.3 to $0.4 million — the lowest in six years, and stable since Q3 2024. VaR-to-FYP is around 0.6%, similar to 2020–2021 levels.

Now, someone might look at $0.3 million and think — that's small, but is the agency just too small to generate risk? Fair question. Let me address that directly.

Yes, the business is smaller: FYP is around $53 million, manpower is about a quarter of prior years, UM+ count is down to about 2,000. And commission rates were reduced to around 26% from H2 2024, down from 32%.

But here's what matters: the quality indicators are strong. Core agents and new agents still contribute about 78% of FYP — same as the 2020–2021 stable period. And 13-month persistency improved to over 84%, up from around 50% in 2023, back in line with the 80% we saw in 2020–2021.

So the low VaR isn't just a function of small scale. It reflects a cleaner, higher-quality agency force with better persistency and more disciplined compensation. The risk profile is genuinely improved.

**[Slide: VaR 2025 Summary]**


Let me give you the current numbers.

Total VaR ending 2025 is $0.29 million — that's 0.6% of agency FYP. Well below the approved trigger threshold of $3 million.

Of that $0.29 million:
- About $0.05 million comes from calculation-gap cases — where production is zero or negative but payout is positive. These aren't risk cases. They're caused by timing gaps between when production happens and when payment occurs — like deferred retention bonuses — and by the formula gap where income reflects the whole team but FYP only captures direct team.
- After removing those, actual VaR is about $0.24 million.

There are 260 UM+ units contributing to positive VaR. Let me show you who they are.

**[Slide: Units with VaR > 0 — Profile]**

Among those 260 units:
- 63% are not GTF — they're organic leaders
- 95% are not top-tier MBA Pro
- 87% — that's 226 units — have VaR under $2,000 for the full year

Think about that: $2,000 per year. That's not a number that encourages gaming behavior. It's not worth the risk of losing your career over $2,000.

These 226 low-VaR units contribute $117,000 — about 40% of total VaR. The remaining 34 units with VaR above $2,000 account for $176,000, and 18 of those have VaR above $4,000.

**[Slide: Deep-dive — Units with FYP > 0]**

Of the 168 units that have both positive FYP and positive payout:
- 76 units have good persistency (P13 above 60%) — these are legitimate producers, low risk
- 16 units have low persistency but their VaR is under $2,000 each — not material
- 76 units don't have P13 yet because they're newly promoted UMs or new GTF appointments. Of these, 71 have VaR under $2,000. Only 5 have VaR above $4,000.

Those 5 units — let me address them directly. All 5 are GTF UM+, 4 out of 5 are MBA Pro. Their payout is 90% from UM+ compensation (31%) and GTF subsidy (57%). The elevated VaR is driven by the calculation gap: their bonus payout is based on direct report team FYP (average $18,950), but VaR uses direct team FYP (average $1,680). It's a scope mismatch in the formula, not arbitrage behavior.

All 5 remain under close monthly monitoring with deep-dive reviews and remediation actions as needed.

**[Slide: Special Cases — Zero and Negative FYP]**

The remaining 92 units all have zero or negative FYP but positive payout. Profile: 100% non-MBA, 55% GTF, and 96% have VaR under $2,000.

The drivers are straightforward:
- Timing gaps — deferred payments from prior-year production like retention bonuses and contest payouts
- Income scope — payout includes override and GTF subsidy from indirect team, but FYP only captures direct team

These are structural features of how compensation flows through the hierarchy, not risk signals. And all 92 units are subject to the same monthly monitoring framework.

**Bottom line on current status:** VaR is at a six-year low, the agency force is cleaner and higher quality than it's been in years, and the cases that do show positive VaR are overwhelmingly explained by formula mechanics rather than gaming behavior. Our existing controls — monthly multi-indicator screening by Line1B, deep-dive reviews, suspension of payments for high-risk cases — remain fully in place.

## PART 3: 2026 Threshold Proposal — Why $3 Million (Slides 6–12)

**[Slide: Compensation Change Impact — Back-test & Forecast]**


Now, looking ahead. Three things are changing in 2026 that will push VaR higher — and none of them mean higher risk.

**Change 1: New compensation scheme** launched January 2026. We back-tested it against actual 2025 performance. Result: VaR would have gone from $0.24 million to about $0.5 million, and units with positive VaR from 168 to 202. The new scheme rewards top performers more — especially MBA Pro UM+, whose VaR surged 2.3 times. Non-MBA VaR increased 52%. This is by design: we want to incentivize high productivity.

**Change 2: FYP growth target of +39%.** When we layer the growth forecast onto the new scheme, VaR rises to about $0.9 million with 306 units showing positive VaR. Higher productivity among top performers and new recruit inflow drive this. Again — growth in VaR driven by growth in business, not by increased risk.

**[Slide: New ILP Product Impact]**

**Change 3: New ILP product (EverGreen)** launched March 2026. This is the big one.

The minimum premium is 2.5 times our current average case size — 50 million VND minimum versus our agency average of 20.3 million as of December 2025. This product is projected to contribute about 40% of total agency FYP by year-end.

The impact: average productivity per active agent jumps 41%, from 38.6 million to 54.4 million VND. And we're already seeing it — as of March 23rd, EverGreen contributed 87% of month-to-date FYP and actual case size reached 71.1 million.

Higher productivity means agents hit higher compensation tiers. Under our payout structure, rates rise with productivity. So VaR is forecast to increase from $0.9 million to about $2.5 million, with 436 units showing positive VaR.

This is the natural consequence of a successful product launch driving higher productivity. The VaR increase reflects business growth, not deteriorating risk controls.

**[Slide: $3.0 Million Trigger Point Proposal]**

So here's our proposal: maintain the trigger threshold at $3.0 million.

Let me walk through the rationale.

The $3.0 million is calibrated against our 2026 forecast of $2.5 million. That gives us a 20% buffer above the forecast — about $0.5 million of headroom.

Why do we need that buffer? For the same calculation-gap cases we see today. In 2025, calculation-gap cases accounted for $0.05 million out of $0.29 million — that's about 20% of total VaR. Applying that same ratio to the 2026 forecast gives us the $3.0 million figure.

These gaps are structural:
- Timing differences between when production happens and when payment is processed
- The formula gap where income includes earnings from the entire team hierarchy, but FYP only captures direct team production
- A UM+ with a large indirect team or high-productive direct reports can show positive VaR even without any actual risk

Now, I want to be transparent: it's still early to fully assess the ILP product's impact on sales mix. But based on initial March data and our forecast assuming 40% sales mix from EverGreen, $3.0 million provides appropriate headroom without being excessive.

**For our regional colleagues** — I understand the instinct to keep this number conservative. Let me offer some perspective:

- $3.0 million represents only about 4-5% of projected agency FYP for 2026. During our stable 2020–2021 period, peak VaR-to-FYP was 1.3%. Even at $3.0 million, we'd be well within historical ranges when adjusted for business scale.
- The $3.0 million is a trigger, not a tolerance. If we breach it, the escalation protocol kicks in immediately — deep-dive analysis by the compensation team and Line1B, reporting to Local ARC and Regional RCSC, with remediation plans.
- Setting the trigger too low risks frequent false alarms from calculation-gap cases, which would dilute the signal and create unnecessary escalation noise.

**[Slide: VaR Monitoring Framework]**

Our monitoring framework has three layers:

1. **Annual deep-dive** by ASBD into payout data — breaking down VaR by compensation component and agency segment to review guardrails as part of the annual compensation review.

2. **Monthly screening** by Line1B — multi-indicator review including payout ratios, persistency, sale patterns, phone and bank card sharing, welcome call success rates, and more. Suspicious cases get escalated to Compliance. High-risk cases get payments suspended until investigation is complete.

3. **Escalation protocol** — when monthly VaR exceeds the threshold, the local compensation team and Line1B provide a deep-dive analysis. This isn't a new standalone process — it's integrated into Line1B's existing monthly workflows.

**[Slides: New ILP Product Monitoring — Process and Action, p1–p3]**

For the new ILP product specifically, we've added targeted monitoring measures on top of the existing framework:

- **Customer care calls** — weekly results from BCS, with re-advisory required within 7 days for flagged cases. Agents with repeated issues must refresh licensing content.
- **Audio recording sampling** — for policyholders or agents with multiple policies, cross-checked against M-Pro results.
- **Agent-own-policy tracking** — monthly analysis at agent and location level, screening for agent-to-agent sales patterns.
- **Compensation scheme abuse monitoring** — reviewing locations and agents with high compensation ratios, analyzing policy patterns for production manipulation, cross-checking submissions near cut-off dates.
- **CIF ratio review** — holding income and paying after freelook for agents with high production but below threshold.
- **ROP ratio monitoring** — rechecking audio recordings and FNA/RPQ for agents with high ratios.
- **Payment history review** — screening suspicious card transactions, linking to other risk signals.
- **Complaint log analysis** — trending by agent and location, cross-checking with sales recordings.
- **Social media monitoring** — flagging agents sharing unauthorized product information.
- **Mystery shopping** — separate questionnaire designed for EverGreen, sampling at least 50% of total samples.
- **Vulnerable customer analysis** — assessing if agent portfolios contain disproportionate numbers of vulnerable customers.

These controls deliver results on weekly and monthly cycles, reported through ARC and to Ops and Risk.

## CLOSING

To summarize:

Our VaR methodology measures at the UM+ unit level — where arbitrage risk actually lives. PSM+ is excluded because it's consistently negative, but monitored separately.

Current VaR is $0.29 million — a six-year low. The agency force is smaller but cleaner, with strong persistency and quality indicators. The cases showing positive VaR are overwhelmingly driven by formula mechanics, not gaming.

Looking ahead, three changes — new compensation scheme, FYP growth, and the EverGreen ILP product — will push VaR to an estimated $2.5 million. This reflects business growth and successful product adoption, not increased risk.

We propose maintaining the trigger at $3.0 million — a 20% buffer above forecast, consistent with the calculation-gap ratio we observe today. Our monitoring framework is comprehensive, and we've added specific controls for the new ILP product.

We're confident this threshold gives us the right balance: enough headroom to avoid false triggers from structural gaps, while keeping the threshold tight enough to serve as a meaningful early warning signal.
