Chapter 17: Coming Home

The drive back to Alder Bay took twelve hours. They drove through the night, switching drivers every few hours, stopping only for gas and food. Maya slept when she wasn't driving — deep, restful sleep filled with dreams of light and water.

They arrived in Alder Bay at noon the next day. The town looked the same — quiet, gray, huddled around the harbor. But Maya could feel something different. The air was cleaner. The ocean was calmer. And the strange, heavy feeling that had hung over the town since she arrived was lighter.

The network was stronger. The town could feel it, even if the people didn't understand why.

Ben and Lily were waiting at Alder House. They had kept watch in shifts, sleeping in the guest bedrooms on the second floor. Nothing had happened — no visits from Frank, no strange lights, no disturbances.

"The dreams stopped," Lily said. "Two nights ago. My mom slept through the whole night for the first time in weeks. Same with half the town."

"The tides are back to normal too," Ben added. "And the fish are coming back to the bay. Whatever you did out there, it's working."

Maya felt a wave of relief. The connections were holding. The network was healing.

But it wasn't enough. She still needed to reach the nexus.

She spread Catherine's map on the kitchen table and explained what she had learned at The Deep Place. The seven lights. The ancient consciousness. The nexus beneath the ocean.

"So you need to go underwater," Ben said. "To the bottom of a deep ocean canyon. To touch the center of a network of magical lights."

"Yes."

"And the light gave you the ability to breathe underwater."

"I think so. I can feel something different in my lungs. Something the light put there."

"You think so," Lily repeated. "Maya, you can't just jump into the ocean and hope for the best. If you're wrong—"

"I know. That's why I need to test it first."

They went down to the harbor that afternoon. Maya stood at the end of the dock, looking at the cold, gray water. Sam, Ben, and Lily stood behind her, ready to pull her out if something went wrong.

Maya took a deep breath and jumped in.

The water was freezing. It hit her like a wall of ice, and for a moment, she couldn't think, couldn't move, couldn't breathe. She sank below the surface, her body rigid with shock.

Then the warmth came. Starting in her chest and spreading outward, pushing back the cold. Her lungs, which had been burning for air, suddenly relaxed. She opened her mouth — and breathed.

Water flowed in and out of her lungs, and she could breathe. Not like breathing air — it was heavier, slower, stranger. But it worked. Oxygen flowed into her blood. Her heart beat steadily. She was alive, underwater, breathing.

She opened her eyes. The harbor water was murky, but she could see — the wooden pillars of the dock, the sandy bottom, a school of small fish darting away from her. And she could see something else. A faint glow, coming from below — the light beneath Alder House, shining through the rock and earth, visible even from the harbor.

She surfaced after three minutes. Sam grabbed her arm and pulled her onto the dock. She was soaking wet and grinning.

"It works," she said. "I can breathe underwater."

"That's impossible," Lily said.

"A lot of impossible things have happened this week," Maya said.

They spent the next few days preparing. Ben, who was an experienced fisherman, knew the waters off the coast. He studied nautical charts and identified the underwater canyon that Catherine's map pointed to — it was called the Alder Canyon, about two miles offshore, dropping to a depth of over a thousand feet.

"A thousand feet," Sam said. "Maya, that's deep. Really deep. Even with the breathing thing, the pressure alone—"

"The light will protect me," Maya said. She could feel it — the seven lights inside her, ready to shield her, guide her, sustain her. "I know it will."

"How do you know?"

"Because it needs me to get there. It wouldn't have given me this ability if it was going to let me die."

It was a good argument, but Maya could see that her friends were not fully convinced. She wasn't fully convinced either. But she had no choice. The nexus was the key to everything — to stabilizing the network permanently, to freeing herself from the burden of constant guardianship, to ensuring that the light would be safe for generations to come.

She had to try.

They set the dive for three days later — the day before Frank's deadline. It would be the new moon, when the light was strongest. Maya would have the maximum power available to her.

In the meantime, she continued reading Eleanor's journals. She was near the end now — the final years of Eleanor's life. The entries were shorter, more tired, but still sharp and clear.

January 15, 2023

I am eighty years old today. The light wished me happy birthday — I felt it pulse three times when I woke up, like a heartbeat saying hello.

I am tired. My body is failing. But the light is patient. It knows that Maya will come. It showed me, years ago — a vision of a young woman with dark hair, standing in the basement, her hands full of light. My granddaughter. The one Margaret tried to hide from me.

I have written everything down. I have left the journals, the map, the cave. Everything Maya will need.

I only hope it is enough.

Maya closed the journal. Tears were running down her cheeks.

"I'm here, Eleanor," she whispered. "I came. Just like you said I would."

The house was quiet around her. But beneath the floor, the light pulsed once — warm, gentle, grateful.

---

The night before the dive, Maya couldn't sleep. She sat on the back porch of Alder House, wrapped in a blanket, looking at the ocean. The sky was clear for once, and the stars were brilliant — thousands of them, scattered across the darkness like diamonds.

Sam came out and sat beside her.

"Can't sleep either?" he said.

"Too much thinking."

They sat in comfortable silence for a while. The ocean murmured below the cliff, steady and eternal.

"Maya," Sam said. "Whatever happens tomorrow — I want you to know something."

"What?"

"I'm glad you came to Alder Bay. I'm glad I met you. And I'm glad I got to be part of this... whatever this is."

Maya looked at him. In the starlight, his face was soft and serious.

"Me too," she said.

She reached over and took his hand. They sat like that for a long time, holding hands in the darkness, listening to the ocean.

Tomorrow, Maya would dive into the deep. Tomorrow, she would face the nexus. Tomorrow, everything would change.

But tonight, she was just a woman sitting with a friend, looking at the stars, feeling the warmth of another person's hand.

And that was enough.
